# Scale, Value, and Transfer Plan

## 1. Strategic Objective

Build a trusted non-clinical health and wellness navigation company that begins with the founder's distinctive skill set but grows into an organization with transferable methods, trained people, owned intellectual property, repeatable economics, measurable quality, and the ability to operate without continuous founder delivery.

The long-term objective is not simply to increase workload. It is to increase the company's capacity and worth by creating assets a buyer, manager, employee, licensee, or franchisee can understand and use.

## 2. What the Business Is Building

The company should deliberately build six forms of value.

### A. Trusted Brand

A recognizable promise and reputation that clients and referral partners associate with reliable, ethical coordination—not only with the founder's name.

### B. Repeatable Operating System

A defined client journey, scope boundaries, decision rules, service packages, templates, software, privacy controls, safety protocols, provider-verification method, and quality-review process.

### C. Trained Delivery Capacity

People who can perform defined work to the required standard, know when to stop, and escalate judgment-heavy situations appropriately.

### D. Proprietary Know-How

The company's methods, manuals, training, action-map structure, provider-network method, scripts, checklists, quality standards, and operating data.

### E. Reliable Economics

Evidence of demand, pricing power, recurring revenue, contribution by service, team productivity, client acquisition costs, retention, cash generation, and location-level profitability.

### F. Transferability

Business-controlled contracts, accounts, records, intellectual property, vendor relationships, referral relationships, policies, and management reporting that can pass to a new owner without destroying the operation.

## 3. Founder Advantage Without Founder Dependence

The founder's personal skill set should be treated as source material for the company system.

For each founder strength, document:

- What the founder notices that others may miss
- Questions the founder asks
- How priorities are determined
- How trustworthy options are identified
- How risk and boundaries are recognized
- How clients are reassured without making promises
- How providers and family members are approached
- How stalled situations are moved forward
- What signals require escalation
- What excellent work looks like

Convert that knowledge into:

- Observable behaviours
- Decision guides
- Examples and case studies using fictional or properly de-identified information
- Scripts and question sets
- Templates and checklists
- Review criteria
- Training exercises
- Supervised practice
- Escalation thresholds

The goal is not to turn sensitive client work into a rigid script. It is to distinguish trainable routines from judgment that requires experience or founder review.

## 4. Business Model Options

The company should preserve several growth paths while it gathers evidence.

| Model | Business controls operations? | Capital need | Control over quality | Main advantage | Main risk |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---|---|
| Founder-led practice | High | Low | High | Fastest starting point | Low capacity and founder dependence |
| Employee team | High | Medium | High | Builds internal capability | Hiring, supervision, and utilization risk |
| Company-owned locations | High | High | High | Strong control and enterprise value | Capital and management complexity |
| Managed local teams | High | Medium to high | High | Local presence with central standards | Requires capable managers |
| Licensed method or brand | Medium | Low to medium | Medium | Faster distribution | Quality and legal classification risk |
| Strategic partnership | Shared | Varies | Shared | Access to audience or infrastructure | Misaligned incentives and brand control |
| Franchise system | Defined by agreements and standards | Shared | Requires strong support and enforcement | Expansion using franchisee investment | Significant legal, disclosure, support, and relationship obligations |
| Sale to strategic buyer | Transfers | Buyer-funded | Transfers | Realizes accumulated value | Weak transferability reduces price |

Do not choose franchising simply because it sounds scalable. First prove that another person can deliver the service, that a location or territory has workable economics, and that the company can provide ongoing support.

## 5. Recommended Growth Sequence

### Stage 1: Founder Proof

Prove:

- A specific client problem
- A clear non-clinical service
- Willingness to pay
- Safe and repeatable client outcomes
- Reliable delivery time and cost
- Referral interest

Exit criteria:

- At least 20 completed engagements or another evidence threshold approved by the business advisor
- Consistent scope and package definitions
- Complete client workflow and privacy controls
- Measured time and margin by service
- Documented complaints, incidents, near misses, and corrections

### Stage 2: Assisted Founder Delivery

Delegate lower-risk work such as approved research, scheduling, reminders, directory maintenance, document preparation, and administration.

Prove:

- Role clarity
- Access restrictions
- Training effectiveness
- Review and escalation
- Handoff reliability
- Positive contribution after true labour costs

Exit criteria:

- A second person consistently performs defined tasks
- Quality is verified, not assumed
- The founder spends less time on routine administration
- No unresolved material privacy or safety failures

### Stage 3: Team-Led Service

Train a navigator to own selected client engagements within clear boundaries, with oversight.

Prove:

- The client experience remains consistent
- The company brand—not only the founder—earns trust
- Escalations occur correctly
- The service remains profitable after market-rate labour and supervision

Exit criteria:

- Non-founder delivery has operated successfully for at least two meaningful business cycles
- Quality and financial results meet thresholds
- The founder can take a planned 30-day absence without operational breakdown

### Stage 4: Second Market or Location Pilot

Test one additional B.C. service area under company control or close management.

Prove:

- Local demand
- Referral development
- Provider-network development
- Location-specific licensing and operating requirements
- Central support workload
- Consistent privacy, safety, brand, and quality controls
- Location-level economics

Exit criteria:

- The second location reaches the agreed quality, demand, and financial targets
- Local differences are incorporated into the operating system
- Central support costs are measured

### Stage 5: Choose the Replication Model

Compare company-owned growth, local managers, licensing, partnerships, and franchising using real evidence.

The decision should consider:

- Desired control
- Available capital
- Management capacity
- Training and support burden
- Legal and disclosure requirements
- Quality and reputation risk
- Territory economics
- Buyer attractiveness
- Founder's preferred future role

### Stage 6: Replicate Carefully

Expand in controlled cohorts. Each new location or operator must pass readiness, training, privacy, safety, financial, and quality gates before serving clients independently.

## 6. Standardization Framework

Every repeatable part of the business should be classified as one of the following:

- **Mandatory standard:** Must be performed consistently, such as consent, emergency response, privacy, incident handling, brand claims, and credential verification.
- **Approved method:** A preferred method with defined room for judgment.
- **Local adaptation:** May vary by location, such as provider resources, community partnerships, travel areas, and local pricing within approved rules.
- **Founder or specialist escalation:** Requires senior judgment or qualified professional advice.
- **Prohibited activity:** Must not be performed by the business.

For each process, record:

- Purpose
- Owner
- Trigger
- Required inputs
- Steps
- Decision points
- Escalations
- Required evidence
- Quality standard
- Time expectation
- Privacy classification
- Review cycle
- Version and approval

## 7. Management System

The company should eventually function through four linked levels.

### Governance

Sets purpose, risk appetite, legal structure, brand ownership, privacy accountability, expansion approvals, and sale or succession decisions.

### Central Business Support

Maintains brand, marketing, software, finance, training, quality, provider-network standards, legal/privacy resources, templates, and performance reporting.

### Location or Team Leadership

Manages local staff, capacity, community relationships, service quality, incidents, financial performance, and compliance with the operating system.

### Client Delivery

Performs intake, planning, coordination, updates, closeout, and escalation according to scope and permissions.

No new location should open until responsibility at all four levels is clear.

## 8. Enterprise-Value Drivers

Track and improve:

- Recurring and predictable revenue
- Revenue diversity across clients, packages, locations, and referral sources
- Sustainable margins after paying market rates for all labour
- Low founder dependence
- Documented and protected brand and intellectual property
- Consistent client experience
- Low complaint, incident, and privacy-event rates with strong response evidence
- Employee retention and training depth
- Proven customer demand and referral relationships
- Provider-network quality
- Reliable management information
- Clean contracts, ownership, taxes, licences, insurance, and records
- Ability to add a location without rebuilding the business from scratch

Avoid vanity growth that increases revenue while reducing margin, quality, cash flow, or control.

## 9. Sale-Readiness Design

Build the company so a reasonable buyer can answer:

- What exactly is being purchased?
- Does the company own the brand, documents, software configurations, content, customer relationships, and data it claims to own?
- Are contractor and employee work products assigned appropriately to the company?
- Can contracts and vendor accounts be transferred?
- Will clients and referral sources remain after the founder leaves?
- Can a manager operate the company using existing systems?
- Are financial statements reliable and normalized for founder compensation and unusual expenses?
- Are privacy, safety, legal, insurance, employment, tax, and licensing issues documented and controlled?
- Is growth repeatable?
- What future investment is required?

Maintain evidence continuously. Do not wait for a buyer to discover gaps.

## 10. Franchise-Readiness Design

B.C.'s Franchises Act applies when the legal definition is met, regardless of the label placed on the relationship. A licence or partnership can still require franchise analysis if it includes relevant payments, brand association, and significant operational control or assistance.

Before offering a franchise:

- Prove the business at the founder location.
- Prove non-founder delivery.
- Prove at least one additional market or location where practical.
- Establish protectable brand assets.
- Complete the operating and training system.
- Establish location selection and territory methods.
- Prove realistic unit economics without guarantees.
- Define initial and ongoing support.
- Determine required staffing and owner involvement.
- Establish quality assurance and enforcement.
- Build incident, privacy, complaint, and crisis support.
- Model franchisor economics, not only franchisee economics.
- Obtain specialist franchise, tax, corporate, employment, privacy, and insurance advice.
- Prepare required disclosure and agreements.
- Establish a controlled prospect and disclosure process.

Under the B.C. Franchises Act, a required disclosure document generally must be received at least 14 days before the earlier of signing a franchise-related agreement or paying relevant consideration. The document must be delivered as one complete document and include required prescribed content and material facts. This is a legal workstream, not an administrative template exercise.

## 11. Founder Role Evolution

The founder's role should evolve intentionally:

1. **Expert doer:** Proves demand and the service.
2. **Method designer:** Captures judgment and creates standards.
3. **Trainer and reviewer:** Develops other people.
4. **Team leader:** Manages capacity, quality, and economics.
5. **System steward:** Protects brand, culture, risk controls, and innovation.
6. **Strategic owner:** Directs capital, leadership, expansion, succession, or sale.

At each stage, define what the founder will stop doing, who will take it over, what authority they receive, and how performance will be checked.

## 12. First Twelve-Month Business-Build Priorities

### Quarter 1

- Complete business readiness and controlled client launch.
- Record founder methods during real work.
- Establish business-controlled accounts and asset ownership.
- Begin clean monthly financial reporting.
- Measure time, revenue, direct cost, and quality by service.

### Quarter 2

- Standardize the most common workflows.
- Create role descriptions and training modules.
- Delegate defined low-risk work.
- Measure rework, handoffs, client experience, and founder time.
- Begin brand and intellectual-property review.

### Quarter 3

- Train a second person through observation, simulation, supervised work, and verification.
- Test founder absence for routine operations.
- Refine capacity and pricing.
- Create the location-readiness and partner-development method.
- Begin buyer-style due-diligence review.

### Quarter 4

- Review whether a second-market pilot is justified.
- Complete a normalized annual financial review.
- Assess team-delivery results and founder dependency.
- Choose the next stage: strengthen the core, expand the team, or plan a controlled second location.
- Do not choose franchising until the replication evidence supports it.

## 13. Strategic Scorecard

Review quarterly:

- Percentage of delivery hours performed by the founder
- Percentage of revenue dependent on the founder personally
- Percentage of recurring revenue
- Revenue and gross contribution by service and location
- Average founder review time per engagement
- Staff utilization, retention, and competency status
- Client satisfaction and coordination-burden change
- Referral-source concentration
- Client concentration
- Number of procedures tested by a second operator
- Number of business-critical assets owned and documented by the company
- Privacy, incident, complaint, and quality trends
- Cash runway
- Owner-adjusted operating profit
- Progress toward a 30-day founder-absence test
- Location or franchise readiness status

## 14. Immediate Actions

1. Confirm the intended company name and whether the founder's personal name will be primary, secondary, or absent from the long-term brand.
2. Create the business asset register.
3. Identify the 10 tasks most dependent on the founder.
4. Begin documenting founder decision-making during client work.
5. Establish monthly owner-adjusted financial reporting.
6. Put every domain, account, document, template, and vendor contract under clear business control.
7. Define the first assistant or navigator role.
8. Select one low-risk workflow for delegation and testing.
9. Establish quarterly founder-dependency and sale-readiness reviews.
10. Book corporate, tax, intellectual-property, privacy, employment, insurance, and franchise advice at the appropriate stage.
