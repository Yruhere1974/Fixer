# Sale-Readiness and Due-Diligence Index

## Purpose

Maintain a clean, organized record of the business so an owner, lender, investor, advisor, or qualified buyer can understand it without disrupting normal operations.

Do not place raw client files or unnecessary personal and medical information in a buyer data room. Due diligence should begin with aggregated, de-identified, redacted, and purpose-limited information. Any later disclosure of personal information requires privacy and legal review, appropriate authority, safeguards, and a documented process.

## 1. Corporate Records

- [ ] Incorporation and organizational documents
- [ ] Share and ownership records
- [ ] Directors, officers, and signing authorities
- [ ] Annual filings and resolutions
- [ ] Related companies and agreements

- [ ] Material approvals and governance minutes

## 5. Clients and Revenue

## 12. Sale Process Controls

Before entering a sale process:

- [ ] Define what is for sale: shares, assets, division, brand, or operating rights.
- [ ] Obtain corporate, tax, transaction, employment, privacy, insurance, and intellectual-property advice.
- [ ] Establish buyer qualification and confidentiality procedures.
- [ ] Create staged data-room access.
- [ ] Redact and aggregate sensitive information.
- [ ] Record every person receiving access and every disclosure.
- [ ] Identify contracts requiring consent or notice.
- [ ] Identify employee-continuity obligations.
- [ ] Plan client, staff, provider, and referral communications.
- [ ] Plan transfer, retention, and destruction of information.
- [ ] Maintain business continuity during diligence.

B.C. employment standards may treat employee service as continuous when a business is sold or otherwise disposed of. Asset sales may also have provincial sales-tax and clearance-certificate considerations. Qualified transaction advisors must assess the actual deal.

## 13. Buyer-Readiness Questions

A strong file should let the business answer:

- Can someone operate the business without the founder?
- Which parts still rely on personal goodwill?
- Are all material assets actually owned or transferable?
- Are the financial statements complete and supportable?
- Is recurring revenue durable?
- Are client and referral concentrations acceptable?
- Are privacy and sensitive-information risks controlled?
- Is the team stable and trained?
- Have complaints, incidents, and liabilities been disclosed appropriately?
- Is growth proven or only projected?
- What investment will a buyer need after closing?

## 14. Quarterly Due-Diligence Review

Each quarter:

1. Add new material records.
2. Update the asset register.
3. Check renewals and change-of-control terms.
4. Reconcile reported measures to source records.
5. Remove duplicate and obsolete working copies under approved retention rules.
6. Review founder, client, employee, referral, and vendor dependencies.
7. Record unresolved risks, owners, and target dates.
8. Confirm that buyer materials contain no unnecessary personal or health information.
