# Fixer Plan Crosswalk and Gap Checklist

**Compared documents:**

- `Documents/kelowna-health-wellness-fixer-plan.md`
- `Documents/kelowna-health-wellness-workflow.md`
- `Project_Operating_System.md`

**Comparison date:** July 16, 2026

## Executive Finding

The documents are complementary rather than duplicates:

- The **Fixer Plan** defines the business: market, positioning, services, boundaries, safety, privacy, pricing, marketing, financial model, launch sequence, and performance targets.
- The **Workflow** defines the client-engagement path and its control points.
- The **Project Operating System** defines how work is assigned, tracked, reviewed, escalated, approved, and closed.

The Project Operating System provides stronger project governance, but it does not replace the business-specific requirements in the Fixer Plan. The safest approach is to retain all three and use this crosswalk as the implementation checklist.

## Missing or Under-Specified Items to Add to Implementation

### 1. Business Definition and Positioning

- [ ] Confirm the initial niche: busy adults coordinating care for themselves or an aging parent in Kelowna, including adult children outside the Okanagan.
- [ ] Choose the public-facing title.
- [ ] Approve the one-sentence promise.
- [ ] Define where the word “fixer” may and may not be used.
- [ ] Document geographic boundaries and the conditions for later expansion.
- [ ] Validate market assumptions through 10–15 prospective-client interviews and 8–10 professional interviews.

**Evidence required:** Approved positioning sheet, interview notes stripped of unnecessary health details, and a documented go/no-go decision.

### 2. Scope and Clinical Boundaries

- [ ] Create a one-page “What I do / What I do not do” sheet.
- [ ] Add non-clinical wording to the website, intake script, agreement, and marketing materials.
- [ ] Prohibit diagnosis, clinical symptom assessment, treatment, medication advice, restricted activities, and protected-title use unless separately licensed and insured.
- [ ] Prohibit guarantees of access, recovery, availability, or outcomes.
- [ ] Confirm that capable clients remain the decision-makers.
- [ ] Prohibit undisclosed referral compensation and clinical recommendations based on personal opinion.
- [ ] State clearly that the service is not an emergency or crisis service.

**Owner:** Founder, with B.C. legal and insurance review.

**Acceptance test:** The same scope boundaries appear consistently in every client-facing and staff-facing document.

### 3. Service Packages and Commercial Rules

- [ ] Finalize the Clarity Session, Two-Week Fix, Ongoing Concierge, and Family Update add-on.
- [ ] Define included deliverables, hours, response times, communication channels, deadlines, and overage rates.
- [ ] Establish transparent fixed-fee and hourly pricing rules.
- [ ] Require written approval for third-party costs and out-of-scope work.
- [ ] Define reduced-fee availability without unpredictable discounting.
- [ ] Ensure fees are for time, coordination, and deliverables—not medical outcomes.

**Evidence required:** Approved service catalogue, price sheet, agreement language, and sample invoice.

### 4. Client Workflow Controls

The following controls from the workflow must be built into templates and systems:

- [ ] Collect minimal information during inquiry.
- [ ] Screen scope before selling a service.
- [ ] Screen emergencies before normal intake.
- [ ] Obtain signed agreement, privacy notice, consent, and communication permissions before work or disclosure.
- [ ] Record approved contacts and information-sharing boundaries.
- [ ] Confirm goals, budget, deadlines, constraints, and accessibility requirements.
- [ ] Verify credentials where regulated providers are involved.
- [ ] Present options, costs, risks, responsibilities, and backups for client approval.
- [ ] Obtain approval before material scope or cost changes.
- [ ] Track actions, dates, costs, decisions, and open items.
- [ ] Provide agreed progress reports only to authorized recipients.
- [ ] Confirm retention or secure destruction at closeout.

### 5. Safety and Incident Management

- [ ] Document the response for immediate danger or medical emergencies: call 9-1-1.
- [ ] Document the suicide-crisis response: call or text 9-8-8; use 9-1-1 if safety is immediately at risk.
- [ ] Document referral to HealthLink BC at 8-1-1 or a qualified provider for non-emergency health information.
- [ ] Define the response to possible abuse, neglect, exploitation, impaired capacity, or unsafe living conditions.
- [ ] Require staff to document objective facts without personally investigating.
- [ ] Route symptom and treatment questions to qualified clinicians.
- [ ] Create an incident log and require review within 48 hours.
- [ ] Publish service hours and an after-hours message.
- [ ] Put the emergency protocol in the agreement, intake form, website footer, and staff handbook.

**Launch gate:** No client work until the emergency protocol is operational and has passed a tabletop test.

### 6. Privacy, Security, Ethics, and Consent

- [ ] Appoint a privacy lead.
- [ ] Have a qualified B.C. professional confirm applicable privacy obligations and review the real workflow and vendors.
- [ ] Collect only information required for the agreed service.
- [ ] Record meaningful consent and authorized recipients.
- [ ] Establish permitted communication channels and keep health details out of ordinary text messages where possible.
- [ ] Use business-owned accounts, unique passwords, multi-factor authentication, encrypted devices, and access controls.
- [ ] Create retention and secure-destruction schedules.
- [ ] Create breach-response, privacy-complaint, access-request, and correction procedures.
- [ ] Prohibit recording without express permission from everyone involved.
- [ ] Prohibit identifiable client health information in consumer AI tools.
- [ ] Document and disclose affiliations, gifts, commissions, and conflicts.
- [ ] Prefer a no-referral-commission model.

**Acceptance test:** Complete a fictional-client privacy walkthrough from inquiry through destruction, including a simulated breach.

### 7. Provider and Partner Network

- [ ] Create the full provider-category register.
- [ ] Aim for at least three choices per category where possible.
- [ ] Record service fit, credentials, insurance or licence where relevant, pricing, cancellation rules, wait time, referral requirements, access, language, cultural safety, privacy practices, and verification date.
- [ ] Verify credentials before each first referral.
- [ ] Recheck directory entries quarterly.
- [ ] Record feedback without unnecessary health details.
- [ ] Disclose selection criteria and allow the client to decide.
- [ ] Prohibit “best provider” claims and pay-to-play ranking.

### 8. Business Launch Requirements

- [ ] Choose and register the business name and structure.
- [ ] Obtain the correct City of Kelowna business licence and confirm home-business or mobile requirements.
- [ ] Obtain appropriate commercial general liability, errors-and-omissions, cyber/privacy, and vehicle coverage.
- [ ] Obtain B.C. legal review of agreements, disclaimers, consent, authorization, cancellation, and liability provisions.
- [ ] Obtain accounting advice on GST, bookkeeping, expenses, and worker classification.
- [ ] Establish a business bank account and bookkeeping system.
- [ ] Establish a secure domain, email, phone, password manager, and encrypted file system.
- [ ] Approve privacy, retention, breach, complaint, incident, safeguarding, conflict, and emergency policies.
- [ ] Complete relevant privacy, first-aid, mental-health, trauma-informed, cultural-safety, accessibility, and boundary training.
- [ ] Conduct a fictional-client tabletop test.

**Hard launch gate:** No paying clients until licence, insurance, contract, privacy workflow, consent process, and emergency protocol are ready.

### 9. Marketing and Referral Controls

- [ ] Approve the core marketing message.
- [ ] Create a professional referral sheet and “do / do not do” sheet.
- [ ] Create an accurate founder biography that does not overstate qualifications.
- [ ] Create an anonymized sample action map.
- [ ] Publish privacy, conflict, pricing, and response-time information.
- [ ] Build a prioritized referral list across the categories in the Fixer Plan.
- [ ] Prohibit fear-based claims, medical claims, guaranteed outcomes, invented scarcity, and privacy-invasive testimonials.
- [ ] Establish a privacy-safe testimonial consent and review process.

### 10. Launch, Pilot, and Growth Gates

#### Days 1–30

- [ ] Complete market and professional interviews.
- [ ] Finalize the niche and offers.
- [ ] Confirm licensing, insurance, legal, and privacy requirements.
- [ ] Draft all client and operating forms.
- [ ] Build the first 10 provider categories with at least three options each.

#### Days 31–60

- [ ] Run 3–5 paid, clearly disclosed pilot engagements.
- [ ] Limit each pilot to a defined outcome and two weeks.
- [ ] Track time, questions, handoff failures, privacy risks, and provider response times.
- [ ] Conduct a closeout interview for every pilot.
- [ ] Revise packages, prices, controls, and templates.
- [ ] Launch an accessible website and referral sheet.

#### Days 61–90

- [ ] Meet 20 potential referral partners.
- [ ] Deliver one educational session.
- [ ] Publish three useful local guides without clinical recommendations.
- [ ] Seek privacy-safe testimonials or referrals.
- [ ] Review financial results and choose the offer to emphasize.

**90-day outcome target:** Five completed clients, three recurring clients, 10 active referral relationships, and a safely repeatable documented process.

#### Months 4–12

- [ ] Stabilize the mix of one-off and recurring services.
- [ ] Verify provider listings quarterly.
- [ ] Complete privacy due diligence before adding a client portal.
- [ ] Establish a quarterly advisory circle.
- [ ] Define workload limits and a wait-list trigger.
- [ ] Hire assistance only after workflows, access controls, contracts, and training are mature.
- [ ] Expand geography or services only after the core operation is profitable and safe.

### 11. Financial Controls

- [ ] Replace illustrative assumptions with pilot data.
- [ ] Track revenue, direct expenses, taxes, insurance, professional fees, software, marketing, travel, administration, and contractor costs.
- [ ] Track actual hours and effective hourly revenue by package.
- [ ] Maintain at least three months of operating expenses when feasible.
- [ ] Take advance payment for fixed packages and invoice retainers monthly.
- [ ] Separate authorized third-party expenses from service fees.
- [ ] Do not hold client funds for provider payments without professional legal, accounting, insurance, and control review.
- [ ] Review operating margin, cash runway, and client concentration monthly.

### 12. Scorecard and Review Rhythm

Add these measures to the monthly review:

- [ ] Qualified inquiries by source
- [ ] Fit-call-to-client conversion
- [ ] Average revenue and actual hours per engagement
- [ ] Percentage of action items completed on time
- [ ] Client-reported coordination burden before and after
- [ ] Repeat and referral rate
- [ ] Provider entries verified within 90 days
- [ ] Complaints, incidents, near misses, privacy events, and disclosed conflicts
- [ ] Client concentration and founder capacity
- [ ] Operating margin and cash runway

Do not claim health outcomes as business results. Measure coordination quality, clarity, access, timeliness, and client experience.

| Marketing and referrals | Founder or marketing owner | Legal/privacy review for claims and testimonials | Monthly channel and conversion review |
| Project register | Project lead | Task owners and reviewers | Daily exceptions and weekly governance review |

## Feedback and Escalation Loop

1. Every workstream owner posts completed work, next action, blockers, decisions required, and expected completion.
2. Evidence is linked in the master task register.
3. Reviewers assess deliverables against written acceptance criteria.
4. Findings receive an owner, severity, due date, and verification requirement.
5. Safety, privacy, legal, financial, or irreversible issues pause the affected work and escalate immediately.
6. Scope and cost changes require client or project-approver authorization before execution.
7. Weekly exception reporting identifies overdue work, missing owners, missing next actions, unresolved findings, late dependencies, and overdue decisions.
8. Monthly governance reviews the scorecard, risks, complaints, incidents, capacity, financial position, and upcoming gates.

## Final Pre-Launch Acceptance Gate

Do not accept paying clients until all critical items below are verified:

- [ ] Business registration and required licence are active.
- [ ] Insurance coverage is active and accurately describes the services.
- [ ] Legal scope, agreement, disclaimers, cancellation terms, consent, and authorizations are approved.
- [ ] Privacy workflow, security controls, retention, destruction, breach, and complaint procedures are operational.
- [ ] Emergency and safeguarding procedures are operational and tested.
- [ ] Client intake, action map, incident form, progress report, expense approval, and closeout templates are ready.
- [ ] Service packages, prices, response times, communication limits, and overage rules are published.
- [ ] Provider verification process and initial directory are ready.
- [ ] Financial, invoicing, bookkeeping, and third-party expense controls are operational.
- [ ] A fictional-client tabletop test has passed, with all findings corrected and rechecked.
- [ ] The master task register contains owners, reviewers, deadlines, dependencies, acceptance tests, and next actions.

## Conclusion

Nothing in the original Fixer Plan should be discarded. The Project Operating System should govern its implementation, while the client workflow should govern each engagement. This crosswalk supplies the missing bridge: every business-specific requirement is converted into an assignable, reviewable, and verifiable control.
