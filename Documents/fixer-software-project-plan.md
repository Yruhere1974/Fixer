# Software Project Plan for the Kelowna Health & Wellness Navigator

**Working name:** Fixer Operations Platform  
**Location:** Kelowna, British Columbia  
**Planning horizon:** Initial implementation plus the first 12 months of operation  
**Version date:** July 16, 2026

> This is an operational software plan, not legal, medical, privacy, security, or insurance advice. Before choosing software or accepting client information, have qualified B.C. privacy and legal professionals review the actual services, information flows, agreements, vendors, and retention practices.

## 1. Executive Summary

The Fixer operation needs a secure, organized workspace that supports the entire client journey—from the first inquiry through consent, intake, planning, coordination, follow-up, billing, record handling, and closeout.

The software should help the business answer five questions at all times:

1. What has the client authorized the business to do?
2. What needs to happen next, who owns it, and when is it due?
3. What sensitive information is being held, why is it needed, and who can see it?
4. What decisions, disclosures, appointments, expenses, incidents, and changes have occurred?
5. What evidence shows that the service was delivered safely, privately, and as agreed?

This should not be treated as a conventional sales database with medical notes added to it. The service will handle personal information and may handle sensitive health, family, financial, accessibility, and safeguarding information. The software must therefore be selected and operated as a controlled client-information environment.

The recommended approach is a small, integrated set of business tools with one clearly designated client record, consistent permissions, written information-handling rules, and as little duplication as possible. The business should avoid an unnecessary collection of disconnected applications because each additional copy, login, integration, message channel, and export creates another opportunity for information to be lost, outdated, disclosed incorrectly, or retained too long.

## 2. Project Objectives

The software project will create an operating environment that:

- Supports the complete non-clinical client workflow.
- Keeps clients in control of decisions and information sharing.
- Makes every task, deadline, approval, and unresolved item visible.
- Protects personal and health-related information throughout its life cycle.
- Separates client-service records from marketing information wherever practical.
- Records consent, authority, purpose, changes, disclosures, and withdrawals.
- Helps the business collect only the information required for the agreed service.
- Supports provider research and verification without turning the directory into a pay-to-play referral system.
- Provides incident, complaint, privacy, and safeguarding workflows.
- Supports invoicing and approved expenses without unnecessarily exposing health details.
- Produces clear client updates, closeout packages, and management reports.
- Supports access, correction, retention, return, and secure-destruction processes.
- Creates evidence for internal reviews, professional reviews, insurance needs, and regulatory inquiries.
- Can grow carefully without weakening privacy, quality, or founder oversight.

## 3. Project Principles

Every software and process decision should follow these principles.

### 3.1 Collect Less

Only collect information that is needed for a defined and reasonable purpose. Do not collect detailed medical histories merely because the software has a field for them.

### 3.2 Use Information Only as Authorized

The system should make the purpose of collection and the client's permissions visible at the point where information is used or shared.

### 3.3 Give Access Based on Responsibilities

People should see only the information they require for their assigned work. Access should not be granted simply because someone works for the business.

### 3.4 Keep One Reliable Record

The business should designate an official location for the current client record. Staff should not have to guess whether the latest information is in email, text messages, a spreadsheet, personal notes, or another application.

### 3.5 Make Actions Traceable

Important additions, changes, approvals, disclosures, downloads, and deletions should be attributable to a person and date.

### 3.6 Keep the Client in Control

The system must support informed decisions, clear choices, consent limits, and withdrawal of permission. Silence must never be treated as approval.

### 3.7 Separate Coordination From Clinical Care

The system should reinforce that the Fixer provides non-clinical research, organization, logistics, and advocacy support. It should not encourage diagnosis, clinical assessment, treatment planning, or medical recommendations.

### 3.8 Design for Problems, Not Only Normal Work

The software must support missed deadlines, unavailable providers, withdrawn consent, complaints, privacy breaches, emergencies, staff departures, service outages, and incorrect information—not just the ideal client journey.

### 3.9 Require Evidence Before Completion

Tasks should not be marked complete without an appropriate result, such as a confirmed appointment, client approval, delivered plan, verified correction, or closeout acknowledgement.

## 5. People and Roles the Software Must Support

The software should recognize distinct responsibilities rather than offering one shared account.

### Client

The client should be able to:

- Understand what information is requested and why.
- Provide or decline consent.
- Identify approved contacts and information-sharing limits.
- Review and approve plans, costs, and material changes.
- Provide documents securely.
- Receive agreed updates and final records.
- Ask for access to or correction of personal information.
- Withdraw permissions, subject to clearly explained limits.

### Founder or Lead Navigator

The founder is accountable for service quality, scope, client decisions, escalations, and completion. The founder may need broad operational access but should still follow the same access and logging rules.

### Assigned Navigator or Assistant

An assigned worker should see only the clients and information required for assigned tasks. Access should be limited further when a person only handles scheduling, provider research, bookkeeping, or administration.

### Privacy Lead

The privacy lead needs oversight of:

- The inventory of personal information
- Purposes and consent practices
- Access rights
- Vendor handling of information
- Privacy complaints and requests
- Breach response
- Retention and destruction
- Training and periodic reviews

The public should be able to find the privacy lead's role title and contact information.

### Reviewer or Approver

A reviewer must be able to examine a deliverable against acceptance criteria without automatically receiving unrelated client details.

### Bookkeeper or Accountant

Financial personnel should receive the minimum information required to bill, reconcile, and report. Detailed health or family information should not appear on invoices or routine accounting records.

### External Professional Advisor

Legal, privacy, insurance, or security reviewers may occasionally require controlled access to specific materials. Access should be time-limited, purpose-specific, documented, and removed afterward.

### Referral Partner or Provider

Providers and partners should not have open access to the client workspace. Information should be shared case by case, for an approved purpose, using the minimum necessary content.

## 6. Required Software Capabilities

The business does not necessarily need one product for everything. It does need the following capabilities to operate as one controlled system.

### 6.1 Inquiry and Relationship Management

The inquiry area should record:

- Inquiry date and source
- Prospective client's name and preferred contact method
- Minimal contact information
- General reason for inquiry without unnecessary health detail
- Location and service eligibility
- Urgency or safety-screening outcome
- Fit decision and reason
- Package discussed
- Follow-up date
- Whether the inquiry became a client
- Reason an inquiry did not proceed
- Referral outcome when the request was outside scope

Sensitive details should not be collected merely to qualify a sales opportunity. Marketing messages and newsletters should be managed separately from client-service permissions.

### 6.2 Scope and Safety Screening

The system should guide the worker through a short, consistent screen before normal intake.

It should record:

- Whether the request is within the non-clinical scope
- Whether there is an immediate medical or safety concern
- Which emergency or professional resource was provided
- Objective facts and action taken
- Who handled the screen and when
- Whether the inquiry was closed, referred, or permitted to proceed

The screen should prominently display the approved 9-1-1, 9-8-8, 8-1-1, clinical-question, and safeguarding instructions. It should never attempt to diagnose the person.

### 6.3 Agreements, Notices, Consent, and Authority

The system must make consent a managed record, not a one-time checkbox.

For each consent or authorization, retain:

- The person providing it
- The information or action covered
- The purpose
- The people or organizations permitted to receive information
- Any information explicitly excluded
- Permitted communication channels
- Effective date
- Expiry or review date where appropriate
- How consent was obtained
- The wording or version accepted
- Withdrawal or change date
- Who recorded the change

The software should prevent or visibly warn against sharing when permission is absent, expired, withdrawn, or narrower than the proposed disclosure.

Separate permissions may be needed for:

- Providing the service
- Contacting a provider
- Booking or changing appointments
- Receiving information from a provider
- Sharing updates with family members
- Attending an appointment
- Handling documents
- Using a testimonial
- Receiving marketing communications

### 6.4 Structured Intake

The intake area should collect information according to the service being delivered. It should be possible to leave irrelevant areas blank.

Possible information includes:

- Client identity and contact details
- Preferred name, pronouns, language, and communication method
- Accessibility and accommodation requirements
- General service objective
- Top priorities
- Deadlines and important dates
- Budget range and expense limits
- Existing providers and services relevant to the assignment
- Approved family members or support people
- Transportation, mobility, scheduling, and location constraints
- Cultural or personal preferences relevant to service selection
- Documents provided by the client
- Known risks relevant to safe coordination
- Information the client does not want shared

Detailed diagnoses, medications, symptoms, clinical histories, government identifiers, or financial-account information should not be collected unless clearly necessary, authorized, and professionally reviewed as part of the service design.

### 6.5 Client Plan and Work Management

Each client should have a current action plan showing:

- Desired outcome
- Priority
- Action
- Accountable owner
- Reviewer where needed
- Due date
- Status
- Dependency
- Estimated or approved cost
- Client approval status
- Backup option
- Evidence of completion
- Next action

The software should support reminders, overdue flags, recurring checks, and an exception view containing:

- Overdue actions
- Actions due soon without progress evidence
- Blocked work
- Missing approvals
- Expiring permissions
- Unverified providers
- Unresolved review findings
- Pending client decisions
- Records approaching a retention or destruction date

### 6.6 Provider and Community-Service Directory

The provider directory should support objective research and client choice.

For each provider or organization, retain:

- Name and contact information
- Service category
- Exact services and client fit
- Location, service area, virtual options, parking, and transit
- Accessibility information
- Languages and cultural-safety considerations
- Pricing and cancellation policy
- Wait time and referral requirements
- Registration or licence details where applicable
- Source and date of credential verification
- Relevant business licence or insurance information when required by policy
- Privacy and communication practices
- Conflict, affiliation, gift, commission, or referral-fee disclosure
- Last general review date
- Next review date
- Status: active, pending verification, stale, restricted, or inactive

The directory should keep factual selection criteria separate from client feedback. Client feedback should not contain unnecessary health details. A provider whose information is stale should not be presented as verified until reviewed again.

### 6.7 Appointment and Logistics Coordination

The coordination area should support:

- Appointment requests and confirmations
- Client authority to book or change appointments
- Provider contact details
- Date, time, location, and accessibility arrangements
- Transportation and companion arrangements
- Required documents and preparation
- Client questions for the provider
- Cancellation deadlines and possible fees
- Reminder preferences
- Outcome limited to coordination facts, not clinical interpretation
- Follow-up actions

Calendar entries and reminders should reveal no more sensitive information than necessary. A reminder may say “appointment” rather than displaying a diagnosis or detailed purpose.

### 6.8 Secure Communication and Family Updates

The software should support approved communication with clients, providers, and authorized family members.

It should make visible:

- Recipient identity
- Authority to communicate
- Approved purpose
- Permitted channel
- Information included
- Date sent
- Sender
- Delivery or acknowledgement status where available

Family updates should follow a standard structure:

- Completed actions
- Upcoming appointments or deadlines
- Open decisions
- Approved expenses
- Obstacles requiring attention
- Information intentionally withheld because it is outside the recipient's permission

Ordinary text messaging should not be the default place for detailed health information. Personal email accounts, shared family threads, and informal messaging applications should not become unofficial client records.

### 6.9 Documents and Records

The document area should provide:

- A consistent client folder structure
- Clear document titles and dates
- Document type and purpose
- Source
- Current or superseded status
- Access restrictions
- Retention category
- Destruction or review date
- A record of major downloads, shares, replacements, and deletions

Examples include agreements, consent forms, client-provided records, action plans, provider shortlists, appointment-preparation sheets, expense approvals, progress summaries, incident records, and closeout packages.

The system should distinguish between:

- A client-provided source document
- The navigator's coordination notes
- A final approved deliverable
- A temporary working copy
- A record required for legal, insurance, complaint, or financial purposes

### 6.10 Decisions, Changes, and Disclosures

The system should maintain separate but connected records for:

#### Decisions

- Question requiring decision
- Options presented
- Decision made
- Decision-maker
- Date
- Reason where appropriate
- Affected tasks

#### Scope or Cost Changes

- Requested change
- Requester
- Reason
- Service, schedule, cost, and privacy impact
- Approval or rejection
- Tasks and documents updated

#### Information Disclosures

- Client concerned
- Recipient
- Information disclosed
- Purpose
- Authority or consent relied upon
- Method
- Date and sender
- Any follow-up or correction

This creates a clear chain between what the client approved and what the business actually did.

### 6.11 Expenses, Invoices, and Payments

The financial area should manage:

- Selected service package
- Price and payment schedule
- Included and additional hours
- Approved out-of-scope work
- Third-party expense request and client approval
- Invoice number, issue date, due date, and payment status
- Refund or credit decisions
- Tax treatment as professionally determined
- Amounts owing without unnecessary service detail

Invoices should use neutral descriptions. Detailed health, family, or provider information should not be copied into accounting software when a simple service description is sufficient.

The first version should not hold client funds for provider payments. If the business later considers that service, it requires a separate legal, accounting, insurance, fraud-control, reconciliation, and authorization project.

### 6.12 Complaints, Incidents, Near Misses, and Privacy Events

The system should provide one controlled intake point for problems, with restricted access and defined escalation.

Each record should contain:

- Event type
- Date discovered and date occurred if known
- Person reporting
- People and information affected
- Objective description
- Immediate containment or safety action
- Notifications or referrals made
- Severity and escalation owner
- Investigation or review findings
- Corrective actions, owners, and deadlines
- Verification that corrective action worked
- Closure approval
- Related policy, training, provider, vendor, or system change

Incidents should be reviewed within 48 hours under the Fixer operating standard. Privacy-event handling and any notification decision should follow current professional advice and approved procedures.

### 6.13 Privacy Requests and Client Rights

The system should support requests to:

- Access personal information
- Understand how information has been used or disclosed
- Correct inaccurate information
- Withdraw consent
- Complain about information handling
- Receive an export or final copy where appropriate

For each request, track:

- Requester identity and verification
- Request type and scope
- Date received
- Assigned owner
- Required response date as determined by approved policy
- Records searched
- Information provided, corrected, withheld, or escalated
- Reason and professional advice where needed
- Date completed
- Client communication

Original information should not be silently overwritten when a correction request affects accountability. The approved process should preserve what changed, when, and why.

### 6.14 Closeout, Retention, and Destruction

At closeout, the system should guide the navigator through:

- Confirming the agreed outcome and remaining items
- Providing the final action summary and provider list
- Confirming outstanding appointments, costs, and decisions
- Returning client-supplied originals where required
- Exporting the client's approved final package
- Recording feedback and complaints
- Ending active permissions that should not continue
- Scheduling retention and destruction actions
- Confirming whether the client is closed, paused, or continuing under a new agreement

The system should assign each information category a reviewed retention rule. It should not use “keep everything forever” as the default. It must also prevent routine destruction where information is subject to a complaint, access request, legal hold, insurance matter, or other approved exception.

## 7. Information and Data Requirements

### 7.1 Information Classification

The business should classify information so workers understand its sensitivity and handling rules.

| Class | Examples | Handling expectation |
|---|---|---|
| Public | Published website content, public provider information | May be shared publicly after approval |
| Internal | Procedures, general templates, business plans | Limited to workers and approved advisors |
| Confidential business | Pricing analysis, contracts, financial reports, provider concerns | Restricted to people with a business need |
| Personal information | Names, contact details, family relationships, invoices, preferences | Protected and used only for defined purposes |
| Highly sensitive personal information | Health details, disability or accessibility information, crisis information, identity documents, financial circumstances, safeguarding concerns | Strongest restrictions, minimum collection, and enhanced review |
| Incident or legal hold | Complaints, breaches, safeguarding incidents, legal or insurance records | Very limited access; destruction paused until authorized |

In this plan, “personal information” means information about an identifiable individual. “PII” is a commonly used technical and business term, while B.C. law generally uses “personal information.” Medical and health-related information is personal information and should be treated as highly sensitive. The business should not assume that informal coordination notes fall outside privacy obligations.

### 7.2 Personal Information Inventory

Before selecting software, create an inventory that identifies:

- Every category of personal information collected
- The people the information is about
- Why it is collected
- Source
- Where it enters the business
- Where it is stored
- Who can access it
- How it is used
- With whom it may be shared
- Which vendors handle it
- Whether it may leave Canada and what professional review is required
- How accuracy is checked
- Retention period
- Destruction method
- Responsible owner

The inventory should include client, prospective client, family, provider, referral partner, employee, contractor, and website information.

### 7.3 Minimum Client Record

A normal client record may require:

- Identity and contact information
- Agreement and service package
- Consent and authorizations
- Goals, priorities, constraints, and accessibility needs
- Approved contacts
- Action plan and status history
- Provider options and verification evidence
- Appointment and logistics information
- Approved costs and invoices
- Communications and disclosures necessary to document service
- Client-supplied records needed for the assignment
- Complaints or incidents where applicable
- Final summary and closeout decision
- Retention and destruction instructions

The presence of a possible field does not make collection necessary. The approved intake design should identify which fields are required, optional, conditional, or prohibited.

### 7.4 Accuracy and Source

Important information should show:

- Who provided it
- Date received or verified
- Whether it is confirmed or unverified
- When it should be reviewed
- Any client-requested correction

The business should not present stale provider details, unverified family statements, or incomplete client information as established fact.

### 7.5 Information Relationships

The software should connect information without unnecessarily copying it. For example:

- A consent record connects to the family update it authorizes.
- A provider verification connects to the shortlist presented to the client.
- An expense approval connects to the invoice or booking.
- A decision connects to the changed task and plan version.
- An incident connects to corrective actions and policy changes.
- A retention rule connects to the records scheduled for review or destruction.

## 8. Privacy and Data-Protection Requirements

The business is expected to operate as a private-sector organization in B.C.; its actual legal position and any exceptions must be confirmed professionally. B.C.'s Personal Information Protection Act addresses how private organizations collect, use, disclose, protect, retain, and provide access to personal information. It also requires responsibility for information under the organization's control, policies and practices, a designated responsible individual, reasonable safeguards, and accuracy in specified circumstances.

### 8.1 Accountability

- Name a privacy lead and publish the role's contact information.
- Maintain approved privacy policies and procedures.
- Keep evidence of training, reviews, complaints, requests, incidents, and corrective actions.
- Review the privacy program at least annually and whenever the service or software changes materially.

### 8.2 Purpose and Collection

- State why information is being requested.
- Collect only what a reasonable person would consider appropriate for that purpose.
- Avoid optional sensitive fields unless there is a real operational need.
- Do not reuse client-service information for marketing without proper authority.

### 8.3 Consent and Notification

- Use clear, understandable notices.
- Separate important permissions rather than bundling unrelated activities.
- Record the accepted notice and consent version.
- Make withdrawal and change processes practical.
- Review delegated or family authority rather than assuming it continues forever.

### 8.4 Access Control

- Give each worker an individual account.
- Prohibit shared passwords and shared staff identities.
- Match access to assigned responsibilities.
- Review access regularly.
- Remove access immediately when no longer required.
- Apply additional restrictions to incidents, complaints, identity documents, financial details, and highly sensitive health information.

### 8.5 Secure Handling

- Protect information while stored, viewed, sent, downloaded, printed, exported, backed up, and destroyed.
- Use business-controlled devices and accounts.
- Prevent avoidable local copies and personal-device storage.
- Limit printing and define secure storage and shredding for paper.
- Avoid displaying sensitive details in calendar titles, message previews, and notifications.
- Establish a safe method to verify recipients before disclosure.

### 8.6 Service Providers and Cloud Software

Before approving a vendor, determine:

- What information the vendor will handle
- Why the vendor needs it
- Where information and backups may be stored or accessed
- Which subcontractors may be involved
- How the vendor protects information
- How the business controls access
- What records of activity are available
- How the vendor reports incidents
- How quickly the vendor will support access, correction, export, and deletion requests
- What happens at contract termination
- Whether the business can obtain its information in a usable form
- How deletion from active systems and backups is handled
- Whether the vendor may use client information for advertising, product training, or artificial-intelligence development
- What contract, privacy, insurance, and professional review is required

Vendor assurances should be documented, not assumed from advertising statements.

### 8.7 Artificial Intelligence

No identifiable client personal or health information should be entered into a general consumer AI service.

Any later proposal to use AI with client information requires a separate approval project covering:

- Defined purpose and necessity
- Client notice and consent where required
- Data minimization and de-identification
- Vendor use of prompts and outputs
- Retention and deletion
- Human review
- Accuracy and harmful-output risk
- Access, logging, and incident response
- Contractual protections
- Privacy, legal, insurance, and security review
- A limited pilot with a stop mechanism

AI output must never substitute for clinical advice, qualified professional judgment, client consent, or human verification.

### 8.8 Retention and Disposal

- Establish retention rules by information category and purpose.
- Retain information needed to support decisions for the period confirmed by professional review.
- Stop destruction when a complaint, access request, incident, legal matter, or insurance matter requires preservation.
- Confirm secure deletion across normal workspaces, exports, devices, and vendor accounts.
- Record what was destroyed, under which rule, by whom, and when without retaining the destroyed content itself.

### 8.9 Privacy Breach Preparedness

The software and operating process should support:

1. Reporting a suspected event immediately.
2. Containing further exposure.
3. Preserving reliable facts and evidence.
4. Identifying affected people, information, vendors, and systems.
5. Assessing risk with the privacy lead and qualified advisors.
6. Making and documenting notification decisions.
7. Correcting the immediate cause.
8. Verifying corrective actions.
9. Updating policies, training, software, and vendor controls.

The system should not encourage workers to investigate serious events beyond their competence or authority.

## 10. Vendor and Software Selection Plan

### 10.1 Required Categories

The business will likely need capabilities in these categories:

1. Client and inquiry management
2. Task and action-plan management
3. Secure forms, agreements, consent, and authorization
4. Secure document storage and controlled sharing
5. Business email, calendar, and communication
6. Provider-directory management
7. Invoicing, payments, and bookkeeping
8. Incident, complaint, and privacy-request management
9. Reporting and exception monitoring
10. Backup, recovery, continuity, and account administration

Several categories may be provided by one carefully selected business platform. Consolidation is helpful only when the combined product meets the privacy, access, export, deletion, and workflow requirements.

### 10.2 Selection Questions

For every candidate, document answers to:

- Which business requirement does it satisfy?
- What personal information would it receive?
- Can the business configure different access levels?
- Can every worker have an individual account?
- Can the business see important account and record activity?
- Can consent, approved recipients, and expiries be represented clearly?
- Can information be exported in a usable form?
- Can records be corrected without hiding important history?
- Can retention and destruction actions be managed?
- Can the vendor support privacy and incident investigations?
- Does it create unnecessary copies in email, calendars, or devices?
- Can health details be kept out of routine notifications and invoices?
- What happens if the vendor is unavailable or the contract ends?
- Is training and support understandable to a small business?
- What is the complete cost, including setup, storage, additional users, support, exports, and cancellation?
- What do the contract and privacy terms allow the vendor to do with the information?

### 10.3 Approval Standard

No product should be approved solely because it is popular, inexpensive, described as secure, or marketed to healthcare businesses. Approval requires:

- Written business fit assessment
- Personal-information and privacy assessment
- Contract and vendor review
- Defined owner
- Test results
- Migration and exit plan
- Training plan
- Final approval by the founder and privacy lead, with qualified professional review where required

## 12. Implementation Phases

### Phase 1: Confirm Requirements and Boundaries

**Purpose:** Establish what the service does and which information is genuinely required before looking at products.

Actions:

- Approve service packages and non-clinical boundaries.
- Map the client journey and exceptions.
- Create the personal-information inventory.
- Classify information by sensitivity.
- Identify consent, authorization, access, correction, retention, complaint, and breach needs.
- Define user roles and access limits.
- Define reports and launch gates.

Deliverables:

- Approved requirements document
- Information inventory
- Role and access matrix
- Client journey
- Privacy and safety requirements
- Product evaluation scorecard

**Gate:** Do not select software until the requirements and information inventory are approved.

### Phase 2: Evaluate and Select Software

**Purpose:** Compare products against the approved business and information requirements.

Actions:

- Create a shortlist for each capability.
- Obtain written vendor information and contract terms.
- Demonstrate real Fixer scenarios using fictional data.
- Assess privacy, access, consent, export, retention, deletion, incident, continuity, and cost requirements.
- Identify gaps requiring process controls or product rejection.
- Obtain professional review where required.
- Select the smallest suitable set of products.

Deliverables:

- Completed evaluation scorecards
- Vendor risk records
- Total-cost comparison
- Approved selection and reasons
- Exit and continuity plan

**Gate:** Do not sign a contract or enter real client information until approvals are complete.

### Phase 3: Design the Operating Workspace

**Purpose:** Configure the selected software around the approved workflow.

Actions:

- Create user roles and individual accounts.
- Create inquiry, client, provider, incident, complaint, and request records.
- Build the consent and approved-contact structure.
- Build action plans, status rules, reminders, and exception reports.
- Establish document categories and naming rules.
- Establish retention categories and review dates.
- Create neutral calendar, message, and invoice conventions.
- Configure provider verification dates and stale-record controls.
- Configure reports and dashboards.

Deliverables:

- Configured test workspace
- Record and document dictionary
- Permission design
- Workflow guides
- Report catalogue

**Gate:** Privacy lead and operational owner approve the design before pilot data is loaded.

### Phase 4: Create Policies, Templates, and Training

**Purpose:** Ensure the software is supported by consistent human practices.

Actions:

- Finalize privacy notice, consent, authorizations, service agreement, intake, action plan, disclosure record, expense approval, incident report, complaint record, progress update, and closeout summary.
- Write instructions for communication, document handling, printing, remote work, provider verification, access requests, corrections, consent withdrawal, breach response, retention, and destruction.
- Train every user on non-clinical boundaries, privacy, safety escalation, minimum collection, secure communication, and incident reporting.
- Require acknowledgement and practical demonstration, not attendance alone.

Deliverables:

- Approved policies and templates
- User handbook
- Training materials
- Training and competency records

### Phase 5: Test With Fictional Clients

**Purpose:** Prove that the full system works without exposing real client information.

Test scenarios must include:

- A straightforward Clarity Session
- An ongoing client with an authorized family contact
- A request that is outside scope
- An emergency or safety concern
- A provider whose credentials are stale
- A client who changes the budget or scope
- A client who withdraws family-update permission
- An incorrect record and correction request
- A client access request
- An email addressed to the wrong recipient before sending
- A suspected lost device or privacy breach
- An overdue task and missing approval
- Staff or contractor departure
- Service outage and recovery
- Client closeout, export, retention, and destruction

For every test, record expected outcome, actual outcome, finding, severity, owner, correction, retest, and approval.

**Gate:** All critical and high-risk findings must be corrected and successfully retested.

### Phase 6: Controlled Pilot

**Purpose:** Validate the system with a small number of paid clients under close supervision.

Actions:

- Run three to five defined, time-limited pilot engagements.
- Restrict users and information to what is necessary.
- Review each active pilot at least weekly.
- Track time, confusion, duplicate entry, missed tasks, consent questions, handoff failures, provider delays, complaints, privacy risks, incidents, and near misses.
- Conduct a closeout interview with every pilot client.
- Convert findings into assigned corrective actions.
- Update software, forms, policies, pricing, and training.

**Gate:** Founder and privacy lead approve repeatable operation before broader launch.

### Phase 7: Launch and Stabilize

**Purpose:** Begin normal operation while maintaining heightened oversight.

Actions:

- Activate daily exception monitoring.
- Hold weekly operating reviews.
- Review the monthly scorecard.
- Review access and vendor issues.
- Verify backups, export, and recovery.
- Monitor workload and apply the wait-list threshold.
- Keep a change log for every material software or workflow adjustment.

For the first 90 days after launch, review every incident, complaint, privacy concern, missed deadline, and failed handoff for systemic improvements.

### Phase 8: Twelve-Month Improvement and Growth

Before adding staff, a client portal, artificial intelligence, new locations, corporate services, or materially different packages:

- Reassess scope and information needs.
- Review privacy, legal, insurance, safety, financial, and staffing impacts.
- Update permissions and access roles.
- Test new workflows with fictional information.
- Train users.
- Run a limited pilot.
- Obtain a new gate approval.

Growth must not be used to justify weaker client control, more information collection, or reduced oversight.

## 15. Risks and Responses

| Risk | Operational response |
|---|---|
| Too many disconnected applications | Designate one official client record and minimize copying and integrations. |
| Collecting excessive health information | Approve required and prohibited fields by service package. |
| Family receives unauthorized information | Use recipient-specific consent and verify authority before every update. |
| Worker sees unrelated client records | Apply assignment-based access and review access regularly. |
| Health details appear in calendars or invoices | Use neutral descriptions and minimum necessary content. |
| Provider information becomes stale | Use review dates, reminders, and an unavailable status until reverified. |
| Client changes or withdraws consent | Make permissions editable, time-stamped, and immediately visible. |
| Information remains after closeout | Assign retention categories and scheduled review or destruction. |
| Vendor fails or contract ends | Maintain export, continuity, ownership, and exit procedures. |
| Lost device or incorrect email recipient | Use approved devices, recipient verification, rapid reporting, containment, and breach review. |
| AI tool exposes client information | Prohibit identifiable client data in unapproved AI and require a separate approval project. |
| Tasks disappear during handoffs | Require owner, due date, next action, evidence, and acknowledgement. |
| Founder becomes a single point of failure | Maintain documented procedures, controlled delegated access, continuity contacts, and recovery plans. |
| Growth weakens oversight | Require new approval gates for staff, services, geography, portals, and AI. |

## 17. Source and Professional Review Notes

This plan uses the following official B.C. sources as its baseline:

- [B.C. Personal Information Protection Act](https://www.bclaws.gov.bc.ca/civix/document/id/complete/statreg/00_03063_01)
- [Province of B.C. — Protecting personal information](https://www2.gov.bc.ca/gov/content/employment-business/business/managing-a-business/running-a-business/protect-personal-information)
- [Office of the Information and Privacy Commissioner for B.C. — Resources for private organizations](https://www.oipc.bc.ca/for-private-organizations/)
- [Office of the Information and Privacy Commissioner for B.C. — Privacy management program resources](https://www.oipc.bc.ca/about/oipc-privacy-management-program/)
- [Office of the Information and Privacy Commissioner for B.C. — Privacy rights](https://www.oipc.bc.ca/for-the-public/what-are-my-rights/)

The B.C. Personal Information Protection Act is current on the official B.C. Laws site to February 3, 2026. It states, among other requirements, that organizations are responsible for personal information under their control, must designate responsibility for compliance, must develop and follow necessary policies and practices, must make reasonable efforts regarding accuracy in specified circumstances, and must use reasonable security arrangements to protect personal information.

The Office of the Information and Privacy Commissioner describes a privacy-management program as including organizational commitment, a privacy officer, reporting structures, a personal-information inventory, policies, risk assessment, training, breach response, service-provider management, and ongoing review.

These sources establish a planning baseline, but they do not determine every obligation or retention period for this particular business. Before implementation, qualified professionals should confirm:

- Which privacy laws and contractual requirements apply to every service and information flow
- The effect of working with public bodies, regulated providers, insurers, employers, or clients outside B.C.
- Consent and authority requirements for family members, representatives, minors, and capacity concerns
- Appropriate retention periods and legal holds
- Breach assessment and notification procedures
- Cross-border processing and vendor-contract requirements
- Whether any software or planned activity changes the non-clinical nature of the service

## Final Outcome

The completed software environment should feel simple to the client and disciplined behind the scenes. A client should experience clear requests, informed choices, reliable follow-through, limited and respectful information collection, appropriate updates, and an orderly closeout. The business should be able to demonstrate who did what, under whose authority, using which information, for what purpose, with what result, and what happened to the records afterward.
