# Kelowna Health & Wellness Fixer Plan

**Working concept:** A trusted, non-clinical health and wellness concierge who helps people turn an overwhelming situation into a clear, manageable plan—finding appropriate providers, arranging appointments and practical supports, preparing questions, organizing information, and following up.

**Location:** Kelowna, British Columbia  
**Planning horizon:** First 12 months  
**Version date:** July 16, 2026

> This is a business and operating plan, not legal or medical advice. Before launching, confirm the business model, contracts, insurance, privacy practices, and scope with qualified B.C. professionals.

## 1. The opportunity

Health and wellness services are fragmented. A person may need to coordinate a family doctor or nurse practitioner, physiotherapy, counselling, nutrition, fitness, transportation, home help, and family communication—all while tired, stressed, injured, aging, or supporting someone else.

The fixer does not diagnose or treat. The fixer makes the system easier to navigate and helps the client follow through.

Kelowna is a promising market because it combines a growing older-adult population, active families, newcomers, seasonal residents, and people managing chronic conditions. The City projects that residents over 65 will account for 24% of the population by 2040 and has identified age-friendly, accessible services as a priority.

## 2. Positioning

### Simple promise

**“I help you figure out what needs to happen, connect you with qualified help, and make sure the details do not fall through the cracks.”**

### Recommended public-facing title

Use a title such as:

- Health & Wellness Navigator
- Personal Wellness Concierge
- Care Coordination Consultant
- Health Logistics & Advocacy Support

“Fixer” is memorable in conversation but can sound secretive or imply that outcomes can be guaranteed. Use it as brand personality rather than the formal service title.

### Initial niche

Start with **busy adults coordinating care for themselves or an aging parent in Kelowna**, including adult children who live outside the Okanagan. This group has a clear problem, values reliability, and can benefit from both local knowledge and regular updates.

Expand later into post-procedure support, newcomer navigation, workplace wellness logistics, and recovery/wellness retreats.

## 3. Scope of service

### What the fixer can do

- Hold a structured intake and clarify the client's goals, constraints, budget, accessibility needs, and deadlines.
- Research suitable regulated professionals, community services, recreation programs, transportation, meal support, and other practical options.
- Verify credentials through the relevant public regulatory-college register when a regulated professional is requested.
- Build a written action plan with priorities, owners, dates, costs, and backup options.
- Book appointments with the client's informed permission.
- Prepare a concise question list for the client to take to an appointment.
- Attend appointments as a note-taker or support person when the client and provider consent.
- Organize records supplied by the client and maintain a current provider/contact list.
- Coordinate non-clinical logistics: transportation, grocery or meal help, fitness options, home organization, reminders, and family updates.
- Check in, document progress, identify obstacles, and revise the action plan.

### What the fixer must not do unless separately licensed and insured

- Diagnose, assess symptoms clinically, prescribe, recommend changing medication, or provide treatment.
- Perform a restricted health activity or imply registration with a professional college.
- Use a protected professional title or present personal opinions as clinical advice.
- guarantee access, recovery, appointment availability, or a particular health outcome.
- Choose for a capable client, pressure consent, or conceal a financial relationship.
- Receive undisclosed referral fees or let commissions influence recommendations.
- act as an emergency or crisis service.

The B.C. Health Professions and Occupations Act restricts certain activities and professional titles. Every marketing page, intake script, and contract should clearly describe the service as **non-clinical navigation and coordination**.

## 4. Signature offers

### A. Clarity Session — CAD $175–$250

A 75–90 minute intake plus a written next-step map delivered within two business days.

Deliverables:

- stated goal and top three priorities;
- recommended categories of support, not diagnoses;
- 3–5 verified options where appropriate;
- questions to ask providers;
- estimated costs, timelines, and next actions.

### B. Two-Week Fix — CAD $600–$900

For a defined situation such as arranging post-procedure logistics, rebuilding a wellness routine, or coordinating an older parent's appointments.

Includes the Clarity Session, research, booking assistance, one appointment-preparation brief, logistics coordination, and two weeks of follow-up.

### C. Ongoing Concierge — CAD $750–$1,500 per month

For clients or families who need continuous coordination. Define included hours, response times, communication channels, and overage rates. Do not promise 24/7 coverage.

### D. Family Update Add-on — CAD $150–$300 per month

A scheduled, consent-based summary for approved family members. Use a standard report covering completed actions, upcoming appointments, open decisions, and expenses. Never share information without explicit client authority.

### Pricing principles

- Charge for time, coordination, and deliverables—not successful medical outcomes.
- Use transparent flat fees for well-defined packages and an hourly rate for out-of-scope work.
- Require written approval before incurring third-party costs.
- Offer a small number of reduced-fee spaces instead of discounting unpredictably.

## 5. Client journey

1. **Fit call (15 minutes):** Explain scope, urgency limits, fees, and what the client wants solved.
2. **Agreement and consent:** Sign the service agreement, privacy notice, communications consent, and any third-party authorization.
3. **Structured intake:** Capture goals, current providers, accessibility needs, budget, risks, deadlines, and permitted contacts.
4. **Triage:** If there is an emergency or crisis, stop normal intake and direct the person to the appropriate emergency resource.
5. **Action map:** Create a one-page plan showing priority, action, owner, due date, status, and backup.
6. **Execution:** Research, verify, coordinate, book, prepare, and document.
7. **Follow-through:** Send agreed reminders and a weekly summary; escalate stalled decisions to the client.
8. **Closeout:** Provide a final summary, securely return or dispose of records according to policy, request feedback, and offer maintenance support only if useful.

## 6. Safety and escalation protocol

Put this protocol in the contract, intake form, website footer, and staff handbook.

- **Immediate danger or medical emergency:** Call 9-1-1.
- **Suicide crisis or concern about someone:** Call or text 9-8-8, available 24/7/365; call 9-1-1 if safety is immediately at risk.
- **Non-emergency health information:** Direct the client to HealthLink BC at 8-1-1 or an appropriate licensed provider.
- **Possible abuse, neglect, exploitation, impaired capacity, or unsafe living conditions:** Do not investigate personally. Document objective facts, follow applicable reporting duties, and obtain legal/professional guidance.
- **Symptoms or treatment questions:** Do not interpret them. Help the client contact a qualified clinician.

Maintain an incident log and review every incident within 48 hours. The service is not an emergency line; publish response hours and an after-hours message.

## 7. Trust, ethics, and privacy

Health information is highly sensitive. B.C.'s Personal Information Protection Act applies to private-sector organizations that collect, use, or disclose personal information.

Minimum operating standard:

- Appoint a privacy lead, even if the founder holds the role.
- Collect only information needed for the agreed task.
- Obtain meaningful consent and record exactly who may receive updates.
- Keep health details out of ordinary text messages whenever possible.
- Use business-owned accounts, strong unique passwords, multi-factor authentication, encrypted devices, and access controls.
- Create written retention and secure-destruction schedules.
- Maintain breach-response and privacy-complaint procedures.
- Never record a meeting or appointment without everyone's express permission.
- Do not place identifiable client health information into consumer AI tools.
- Disclose affiliations, gifts, commissions, and conflicts in writing. Prefer no referral commissions.

Commission a B.C. privacy professional or lawyer to review the actual workflow and vendor choices before accepting client health information.

## 8. Kelowna partner network

Build a curated network, not a pay-to-play directory. Include at least three choices per category when possible.

### Network categories

- Family medicine and nurse-practitioner access points
- Pharmacies
- Registered massage therapy, physiotherapy, occupational therapy, dietetics, psychology/counselling, chiropractic, acupuncture, and naturopathic medicine
- Fitness professionals and accessible recreation
- Home care and respite
- Seniors' supports and caregiver resources
- Mental-health and substance-use services
- Indigenous-led health and wellness services
- Newcomer and language supports
- Transportation and mobility services
- Meal, grocery, housekeeping, and home-safety support
- Legal, financial, and advance-care-planning professionals

### Vetting checklist

For every partner, record:

- exact service and client fit;
- current licence/registration where applicable;
- insurance and business licence where relevant;
- price range and cancellation policy;
- wait time and referral requirements;
- location, parking, transit, virtual options, and accessibility;
- languages and cultural safety considerations;
- privacy and communication practices;
- date last verified;
- client feedback, recorded without unnecessary health details.

Never describe a provider as “the best.” Explain the selection criteria and let the client decide.

### Useful public starting points

- City of Kelowna accessibility and adaptive recreation resources
- City programs for adults 50+
- Kelowna social-wellness and community resource information
- Interior Health services
- HealthLink BC
- Public registers of B.C. health-profession regulatory colleges

## 9. Business setup checklist

Before the first paid client:

- Choose and register the business structure and name.
- Apply for the appropriate City of Kelowna business licence. The City states that Kelowna businesses, including home-based businesses, require a licence; confirm the correct category and any home-occupation conditions directly with Business Licensing.
- Confirm whether an inter-community mobile business licence is relevant.
- Obtain commercial general liability, professional/errors-and-omissions, cyber/privacy, and any required vehicle coverage. Describe the activities precisely to the broker.
- Have a B.C. lawyer review the service agreement, disclaimer, consent/authorization forms, cancellation terms, and liability provisions.
- Have an accountant confirm GST obligations, expense treatment, bookkeeping, and contractor/employee classification.
- Create privacy, retention, breach, complaints, incident, safeguarding, conflict-of-interest, and emergency policies.
- Set up a separate business bank account, bookkeeping system, secure email/domain, business phone, password manager, and encrypted file system.
- Complete relevant training: privacy, mental-health first aid, trauma-informed practice, cultural safety, accessibility, CPR/first aid, and boundaries. Training does not expand the legal scope of service.
- Run a tabletop test using a fictional client before launch.

## 10. Marketing plan

### Core message

**Health and wellness coordination for Kelowna families who need a clear plan and a reliable person to carry the details forward.**

### Best early referral channels

- elder-law and estate-planning lawyers;
- accountants and financial planners serving older adults;
- discharge planners and clinic administrators, subject to their policies;
- regulated health professionals who see coordination gaps;
- seniors' organizations and caregiver groups;
- relocation professionals and employers;
- community, cultural, Indigenous, and newcomer organizations.

### Credibility assets

- a one-page “What I do / What I do not do” sheet;
- founder bio with accurate qualifications only;
- sample anonymized action map;
- privacy and conflict-of-interest statements;
- clear prices and response times;
- educational workshops such as “How to organize care for an aging parent in Kelowna.”

Do not market with fear, medical claims, guaranteed outcomes, invented scarcity, or testimonials that reveal sensitive health information.

## 11. 90-day launch plan

### Days 1–30: Define and protect

- Interview 10–15 prospective clients or caregivers about coordination pain points; do not collect unnecessary health details.
- Interview 8–10 local professionals about gaps and referral boundaries.
- Select the older-adult/family-care niche and finalize the four offers.
- Confirm business name, licence category, insurance availability, and legal/privacy requirements.
- Draft the service agreement, consent forms, intake, action map, incident form, and closeout summary.
- Begin the provider directory with 10 service categories and at least three options in each.

**Gate:** Do not accept paying clients until the licence, insurance, contract, privacy workflow, and emergency protocol are ready.

### Days 31–60: Pilot

- Run 3–5 paid pilot engagements at a clearly disclosed introductory rate.
- Limit each pilot to one defined outcome and two weeks.
- Track time spent, client questions, handoff failures, privacy risks, and provider response times.
- Conduct a closeout interview and revise the packages and templates.
- Launch a simple, accessible website and professional referral sheet.

### Days 61–90: Build referrals

- Meet 20 potential referral partners with a concise scope sheet.
- Deliver one free educational session for caregivers or older adults.
- Publish three useful local guides without making clinical recommendations.
- Ask satisfied clients for a privacy-safe testimonial or referral.
- Review financial results and decide which offer to emphasize.

**90-day target:** 5 completed clients, 3 recurring clients, 10 active referral relationships, and a documented process that can be repeated safely.

## 12. Months 4–12

- Reach a stable mix of one-off Clarity Sessions, Two-Week Fixes, and recurring concierge clients.
- Review every provider listing quarterly and credentials before each first referral.
- Add a secure client portal only after completing privacy due diligence.
- Establish a quarterly advisory circle including a regulated clinician, privacy/legal professional, caregiver, and accessibility advocate.
- Document workload limits and use a wait-list before quality declines.
- Consider a carefully screened assistant only after workflows, access controls, contracts, and training are mature.
- Expand geographically or into corporate wellness only after the core Kelowna service is profitable and safe.

## 13. Basic financial model

Use conservative assumptions and replace them after the pilots.

### Illustrative monthly revenue at steady early operation

| Service | Volume | Average fee | Revenue |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Clarity Session | 6 | $225 | $1,350 |
| Two-Week Fix | 4 | $750 | $3,000 |
| Ongoing Concierge | 5 | $1,000 | $5,000 |
| Workshops/add-ons | — | — | $650 |
| **Total gross revenue** | | | **$10,000** |

This is a planning scenario, not a forecast. Subtract taxes, insurance, professional fees, software, marketing, travel, unpaid administration, and contractor costs. Track effective hourly revenue for every package; coordination work can expand quickly unless scope and communication limits are explicit.

### Key financial rules

- Hold at least three months of operating expenses.
- Take payment in advance for fixed packages and invoice retainers monthly.
- Separate client-authorized third-party expenses from service fees.
- Never hold client funds for provider payments unless the legal, accounting, insurance, and control implications have been professionally addressed.

## 14. Scorecard

Review monthly:

- qualified inquiries and their source;
- fit-call-to-client conversion;
- average revenue and actual hours per engagement;
- percentage of action items completed by the agreed date;
- client-reported reduction in coordination burden (1–10 before and after);
- repeat/referral rate;
- partner directory entries verified within the last 90 days;
- complaints, incidents, near misses, privacy events, and conflicts disclosed;
- client concentration and founder capacity;
- operating margin and cash runway.

Health outcomes should not be claimed as the fixer's results. Measure coordination quality, clarity, access, timeliness, and client experience.
